Physics Research at Duke and UNC

    May 2025 - Present Advisor: Dr. Christopher Monroe
    Duke Quantum Center, Duke University
    Project: Magnetic field stabilization for Quantum Networking Experiment

        Trapped-ion quantum computers use atomic energy levels to perform computations. Some these energy levels, such as Zeeman levels, are sensitivve to magnetic field, and if magnetic field noise is present it results in computational errors.
    Project goal was to stabilize magnetic field in the quantum computer using feedback loop. This is somewhat similar to the operation of noise-cancelling headphones: microphone measures the noise and "anti-noise" is feed into the speakers effectively cancelling it for the listener. In my case I used two Hall probes to measure the magnetic field drift/noise. I made the PID electronics that was generating the feedback signal, which was used to drive the coils generating now noise-free magnetic field.
    Things learned: Quantum computring and communication basics, Feedback loops, noise in electronics, circuit design, troubleshooting and implementation for PID controllers, driving power loads: push-pull using transistors. Data acquisition and analysis. PCB design.

    March-May 2025 Department of Mathematics, UNC Chapel Hill
    Advisor: Dr. Sorin Mitran
    Project: Non-regular tilings: topic review
    Download the Review Paper (PDF)
    Abstract: All crystals are made of atoms or moleculs arranged in a lattice. Atomic layers in crystals are arranged in 2D lattice which can be seen as a plane filled with tiles. Tiling of the plane so that no empty space is left has translational and rotational symmetries defined by the lattice type. However, there are tilings that lack translation symmetry while still being non-random. Such tilings are called non-regular.
    In this paper I review non-regular tilings and their applications in condensed matter physics (quazi-crystals)
    Things learned: Solid state crystal lattice fundamentals, crystal lattice types, 2D crystal lattice tilings, non-regular lattice tilings, quazi-crystals and their applications

    July 2024 Advisor: Dr. René Lopez
    Department of Applied Physical Sciences, UNC Chapel Hill
    Project: Material fluorescence study for selective laser-sintering

        3D printing is one of the main ways to manufacture parts. Out of many types of 3D printing selective laser sintering (SLS) allows production of strong and support-free parts. SLS uses infrared laser light to melt and fuse powder particles together to form the wanted part.
        The project I joined was focusing on developing and studying new polymer materials for used for SLS. Pulses of focused infrared CO2 laser light were used to locally heat up and fuse the material. This heating process resulted in powder emitting light, characteristic to the polimer used. This fluorescence spectrum was analyzed by camera and spectrometer.
        At first I shadowed the graduate student to learn about the experiment: equipment, computer control and data collection. Then I helped running the experiment. Later I got my own project: I wrote a python code to analyze the material flourscence (live video feed hystogram for RGB channels).
    Things learned: Selective laser sintering, IR optics alignment, fiber coupling to multi-mode fiber, experiment control, data acquisition, video processing using python
